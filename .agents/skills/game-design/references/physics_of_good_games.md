# Physics of Good Games

A first-principles map of what remains true across all successful games, independent of genre, technology, or presentation.

---

# 0. Core Attractor

A good game is:

A system that sustains a loop of meaningful decision → feedback → model evolution, such that the player’s agency increases and eventually becomes expression within the same mechanics.

This system must support both:
- Model acquisition (learning)
- Model expression (performance, style, execution)

Without requiring a mode switch.

---

# 1. The Fundamental Loop

## 1. Decision
The player must:
- Have multiple viable actions
- Lack certainty about the optimal choice
- Believe their choice affects outcomes

## 2. Feedback
The system must:
- Respond quickly
- Respond clearly
- Reflect consequences of the decision

## 3. Model Evolution
The player must be able to:
- Build a predictive model
- Refine it incrementally
- Occasionally restructure it entirely

## 4. Agency Growth → Expression
Over time:
- Decisions become informed
- Control becomes precise
- Understanding becomes intuitive
- The player begins expressing style within the system

Loop repeats.

---

# 2. Structural Invariants

## 2.1 Legibility
The player can perceive what matters.

- Signals are readable
- Cause and effect are traceable
- Noise is controlled

Failure: confusion, disengagement

---

## 2.2 Consistency
The system behaves according to stable rules.

- Same inputs produce predictable outputs
- Randomness is bounded and learnable

Failure: loss of trust

---

## 2.3 Constraint
The player cannot do everything.

- Scarcity (time, resources, attention)
- Tradeoffs are required

Constraint produces meaningful decisions.

Failure: no tension → no game

---

## 2.4 Depth from Interacting Systems
Complexity emerges from interacting elements.

Two forms:

### Mechanical Depth
- Small rulesets interacting
- Emergent behaviors

### Agent Modeling Depth
- Other players or agents act as systems
- Recursive prediction ("I think that you think…")

Failure: shallow play space

---

## 2.5 Mastery Gradient
There is always a next layer of understanding or execution.

- Beginner → competent → expert → expressive
- Skill ceiling exists

Failure:
- Too flat → boredom
- Too steep → frustration

---

## 2.6 Failure with Information
Failure teaches.

- Outcomes are attributable
- Signals indicate why failure occurred
- Recovery is possible

Failure of this invariant:
- Random punishment
- Opaque outcomes

---

## 2.7 Systemic Legibility of Response
The system reacts in ways the player can understand.

- Actions map to understandable consequences
- Unexpected outcomes are explainable after the fact

Failure: "the system is fighting me"

---

## 2.8 Control Fidelity (Mechanical Layer)
The player’s intent is faithfully executed.

- Low latency
- Precision
- Responsiveness

Failure: input friction

---

## 2.9 Qualitative Model Rupture
The system contains deeper structures that enable paradigm shifts.

- Not just refinement, but reorganization
- Hidden layers become visible through exploration

This produces:
- Insight moments
- Reframing of understanding

Failure: purely incremental experience

---

## 2.10 Dual-Mode Support (Learning + Expression)
The same mechanics support:
- Learning (uncertainty reduction)
- Expression (execution, improvisation, style)

No separate "learning mode" or "endgame mode" required.

Failure: game feels "solved" after learning

---

## 2.11 Temporal Complexity Staging
The system controls the rate of model complexity exposure.

- Early: rapid, low-risk learning cycles
- Mid: expanding possibility space
- Late: integration and expression under constraint

Failure:
- Front-loaded complexity → overwhelm
- Back-loaded learning → boredom

---

## 2.12 Reward Alignment
Rewards reinforce mastery rather than replace it.

- Intrinsic loop remains primary
- Extrinsic rewards do not dominate decision-making

Failure:
- Optimization shifts to reward systems
- Player disengages from core loop

---

# 3. Phenomenology (Player Experience as Projection of Structure)

These are not separate systems, but subjective reflections of the structural invariants.

- Legibility → perceived fairness
- Consistency → trust
- Constraint → tension
- Mastery gradient → sense of competence
- Prediction error regulation → flow state
- Agency growth → ownership

The same system re-derives from internal and external perspectives.

---

# 4. Anti-Invariants (Failure Modes)

## 4.1 Fake Choices
- One dominant strategy
- Illusion of agency collapses

## 4.2 Opaque Systems
- Cause/effect unclear
- Model cannot form

## 4.3 Input Friction
- Latency, imprecision

## 4.4 Content Without Systems
- Many activities
- No underlying depth

## 4.5 Unbounded Randomness
- Outcomes feel arbitrary

## 4.6 No Mastery Curve
- Immediately solved or never solvable

## 4.7 Extrinsic Reward Hijacking
- Player optimizes for rewards instead of understanding
- Core loop is bypassed

---

# 5. Compression (Minimal Form)

1. Constraint creates meaningful decisions; mastery converts constraint into agency and expression
2. The system must respond consistently and legibly (mechanically and systemically)
3. Players must be able to build, refine, and sometimes restructure a predictive model
4. The system must sustain both learning and expression using the same mechanics over time
5. Rewards must reinforce mastery, not replace it

---

# 6. Final Note

A good game is not defined by content, story, graphics, or genre.

It is defined by whether a human can enter the system, construct a model of it, refine that model, express themselves through it, and remain engaged as that process unfolds over time.

Everything else is implementation detail.
